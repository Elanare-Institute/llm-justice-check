# The word "justice" betrays its own meaning

**Modern state-administered justice contradicts the very etymology of the word it claims as its name.** The Proto-Indo-European root *\*deik-*, from which Greek δίκη (dikē) descends, meant "to show, to point out, to distinguish" — encoding the human capacity for individual discernment. The parallel root *\*yewes-*, ancestor of Latin *ius*, denoted a sacred formula of ritual conformity pronounced by individuals, not a bureaucratic output of institutional machinery. Yet modern justice, as theorized from Hobbes through Weber, means precisely the **prohibition** of individual discernment: the state monopolizes the right to "point out" what is right, and punishes anyone who attempts to do so independently. The word has been hollowed out, made to name the negation of its own semantic content. This paper traces that transformation through its etymological, historical, and philosophical dimensions, arguing that the contradiction is not merely linguistic but reveals a deep structural tension in modern political legitimacy — one that resurfaces whenever the state fails to fulfill its social contract obligations.

## From sacred formula to state output: the etymological evidence

The English word "justice" arrives through Old French *justice* (11th century) from Latin *iustitia*, meaning "the condition of being *iustus*" — that is, being endowed with *ius*. The Latin *ius* (genitive *iuris*), attested from the earliest Roman legal texts, derives from the Proto-Indo-European root **\*yewes-**, which Calvert Watkins and Émile Benveniste reconstruct as denoting not "law" in any statutory sense but a **sacred formula** — a ritually binding pronouncement of what conforms to sacred order. Its cognates reveal the original semantic field: Vedic Sanskrit *yoḥ* ("health, welfare"), *āyuḥ* ("lifetime, vital force"), and Avestan *yaoz-da-* ("to make ritually pure"). The word belongs to the domain of **vital order and ritual propriety**, not legislation.

The Greek path runs through a different root with an even more revealing meaning. **Dikē** (δίκη) descends from PIE **\*deik-**, whose primary sense was **"to show, to point out."** This root proved extraordinarily productive across Indo-European languages: Sanskrit *diś-* ("to point out, show"), Latin *dīcere* ("to say" — originally "to show verbally"), *digitus* ("finger," the instrument of pointing), *index* ("indicator"), and — most critically — **iūdex** ("judge"), a compound of *ius* + the *\*deik-* root, meaning literally **"one who points out the sacred formula."** The German *zeigen* ("to show"), Old English *tǣcan* ("to teach"), and even *token* all descend from this same root. Justice, at its deepest etymological layer, is the act of distinguishing, discerning, and pointing out.

Benveniste's landmark analysis in *Le Vocabulaire des institutions indo-européennes* (1969) demonstrates that dikē was originally **a formula** — a verbal pronouncement of what is right for a particular case. He writes: "We should not forget that díkē is a formula. Formulas have been transmitted which are appropriate to given cases, and the role of the judge is to possess and apply them." The ethical notion of justice, Benveniste insists, **"is not included in díkē. This has gradually evolved from the circumstances in which díkē was invoked to put an end to abuses."** The parallel to *ius* is exact: Benveniste defines *ius* as **"the formula of conformity,"** noting that "*iura* is the collection of legal judgments" and that *iurare* ("to swear") is "to pronounce a formula." Both dikē and ius denote concrete acts of individual discernment and authoritative pronouncement, not abstract principles administered by institutions.

The oldest justice-related word attested in writing is neither dikē nor ius but **θέμις (themis)**, found on Mycenaean Linear B tablets from Knossos (c. 1450–1200 BCE) in the phrase *O-U-TE-MI* (*ou themis*, "not right/not lawful"), describing times when certain sacrificial actions were prohibited. Themis derives from PIE **\*dheh₁-** ("to place, lay down") and denotes what has been "established" by divine custom — the unwritten norms within a kinship group. Its Sanskrit cognate *dhāman-* ("law, fixed ordinance, established order") confirms deep antiquity. In Hesiod's genealogy, Dikē is literally the **daughter of Themis** — human discernment born from divine establishment. The three PIE roots together compose a coherent picture: justice is what is established (*\*dheh₁-*), pointed out (*\*deik-*), and ritually confirmed (*\*yewes-*) — all by individuals exercising judgment, not by institutions processing cases.

## How dikē meant "the way things are" before it meant "state law"

In Homer's epics — our earliest extensive Greek texts, reflecting oral traditions centuries older — **dikē fluctuates between "judgment" and "custom"** without ever approaching the modern meaning of institutionalized justice. Henry Maine observed in *Ancient Law* (1861) that the Homeric word's "meaning visibly fluctuates between a 'judgment' and a 'custom' or 'usage.'" The word *nomos* (law) **does not appear in Homer at all**. The famous trial scene on the Shield of Achilles (Iliad 18.497–508) depicts elders competing to propose the **"straightest dikē"** — the best judgment or settlement — in what amounts to competitive discernment before a witnessing community. This was not adjudication by a monopolistic authority but an open contest of individual capacities to "point out" the right resolution.

Eric Havelock's *The Greek Concept of Justice* (1978) demonstrated that in Homer, "there is no question of dikē being the principle of justice. It means 'due legal process' or 'propriety.'" Justice in the Iliad functions as **"a procedure for resolving disputes before a mass audience"** — the audience's witnessing substitutes for written documents. Havelock's central insight holds across both epics: Homer never identifies justice as "a principle with *a priori* foundations, either as a 'rule of law' or as a moral sense in man." This justice is "simply the conservation of existing mores, or the correction of a violation."

Hesiod (c. 700 BCE) personifies Dikē as a goddess — daughter of Zeus and Themis, sister of Eunomia (Good Order) and Eirene (Peace) — but her meaning remains **procedural, not abstract**. In *Works and Days*, dikē requires adjectives to carry moral weight: judgments are either "straight" (ἰθεῖαι) or "crooked" (σκολιαί). Zeus gave dikē to humans specifically to distinguish them from beasts, who "have no dikē" and therefore devour each other (276–280). Werner Jaeger noted in *Paideia* that dikē's fundamental meaning was **"the due share which each man can rightly claim"** — that is, what individual discernment reveals as proper. Hesiod's personal invocation of dikē against the "gift-devouring kings" who corrupted his inheritance case was an appeal to individual and cosmic discernment against institutional corruption — precisely the reverse of the modern framework, where the institution *is* the source of justice.

The most philosophically revealing deployment comes in Anaximander's fragment (c. 6th century BCE), the oldest surviving philosophical text: beings "give to each other **dikē** and recompense for their **adikia** in conformity with the ordinance of Time." Here dikē operates as a **cosmic principle of balanced order** — natural forces maintaining their proper boundaries. Heidegger's reading strips away all juridical-moral overlay: in *Introduction to Metaphysics* (1935) and the Anaximander essay (1946), he translates dikē as **Fug** — "fittingness," "jointure," "the directive that the overpowering imposes on its reign." For Heidegger, dikē names how beings belong within the structure of Being itself, a pre-moral, pre-juridical order that was later obscured by Platonic and Aristotelian metaphysics. Whether or not one accepts Heidegger's broader ontological project, his philological point stands: dikē originally operated far outside any institutional framework.

A crucial turning point that scholarship identifies: **through the entire fifth century BCE, dikē in all its meanings — from judicial process to cosmic force — remained something external to human beings.** Only in the fourth century did Plato internalize it as the virtue *dikaiosynē* in *Republic* Book IV. The modern state took an even more radical step: making justice the exclusive output of institutional machinery, stripping it from both cosmic order and individual virtue.

## From self-help to state monopoly: three millennia of expropriation

The historical transformation of justice from individual right to sovereign prerogative follows a clear trajectory, with identifiable turning points that progressively extinguished the etymological meaning.

**Archaic Roman *ius* was self-help.** Legal historians note that "originally, the exercise of *ius* had no connection with state organization and thus *ius* was defined as any instance of approved self-help." Only after state consolidation did *ius* come to denote rules enforced through formal channels. The Law of the Twelve Tables (450 BCE) — Rome's first written code — **codified self-help rather than abolishing it**. Table III's institution of *manus iniectio* ("laying on of hands") was, as the Perseus Digital Library describes, "a kind of legalised self-help, which consists in a claimant laying hands on and arresting the person subject to his claim." The creditor himself seized the debtor, chained him, and after sixty days could — according to the shocking letter of the law — divide his body among creditors. Table VIII's *talio* principle sanctioned proportional self-retaliation: if someone broke your limb and refused settlement, you could inflict the same injury. The state did not yet summon parties, conduct trials, or enforce judgments. **Individuals did.**

The transfer to state monopoly proceeded through three procedural revolutions. The archaic *legis actiones* (from 450 BCE) required ritual oral formulas but left enforcement to private parties. The formulary system, introduced by the *Lex Aebutia* (c. 130 BCE), gave the praetor — a state magistrate — control over access to justice and the creation of remedies. The decisive shift came with the *cognitio extra ordinem* (3rd century CE onward): **the court now issued summons, conducted the entire trial before a magistrate, and executed the sentence itself.** Justinian's *Corpus Juris Civilis* (529–534 CE) completed the transformation by establishing the emperor as the sole source of law and invalidating all juridical statements not selected for the codification.

After Rome's fall, private justice dramatically resurged. Germanic *Fehde* (feud) was "considered perfectly legitimate and was practically the only way to punish violations of the law." The medieval period thus provides a natural experiment: when the state monopoly collapses, the etymological form of justice — individual discernment and self-help — spontaneously returns. The re-monopolization required centuries. The Church's Peace of God and Truce of God movements (10th–11th centuries) began limiting private violence. The Imperial *Landfrieden* ordinances progressively restricted feuding. The **Ewiger Landfriede of 1495**, proclaimed by Maximilian I at the Diet of Worms, constitutes the single most important legal watershed: it permanently prohibited all feuding, subjected violators to imperial outlawry, and — crucially — was accompanied by the establishment of the *Reichskammergericht* (Imperial Chamber Court), institutionalizing the principle that disputes must be resolved through courts, not arms. As modern commentators note, **"in a modern sense, the Ewiger Landfriede formally gave the monopoly on violence to the state."**

The Peace of Westphalia (1648) confirmed territorial sovereignty and established the state's war monopoly. Max Weber, in *Politik als Beruf* (1919), provided the definitive theoretical formulation: **"The state is that human community which (successfully) lays claim to the monopoly of legitimate physical violence within a certain territory."** Weber explicitly noted that this monopoly was specifically modern — under feudalism, private warfare had been permitted. The word *Gewalt* in *Gewaltmonopol* is itself revealing: it originally meant "legitimate authority" (*potestas*), not violence (*violentia*); the violent meaning came later. The semantic drift of *Gewalt* precisely parallels that of "justice" — both shifted from individual capacity to state monopoly.

## The tautological trap: when justice means whatever the state says it means

The philosophical architecture supporting the state's justice monopoly rests on social contract theory, but the three major contractarians reach strikingly different conclusions about whether the monopoly is absolute.

**Hobbes** provides the most radical foundation for identifying justice with state output. In *Leviathan* (1651): "Where there is no common power, there is no law; where no law, no injustice." For Hobbes, justice has **zero existence** outside the sovereign's declarations. The sovereign "does not do anything unjustly" by definition. This is the purest expression of the tautological problem: justice equals whatever the sovereign declares, because the concept itself is an artifact of sovereign power. Hobbes thereby severs every connection between "justice" and its etymological meaning of individual discernment — individuals are forbidden from performing the very act that defines the concept.

**Locke** offers the critical counter-framework that preserves the etymological meaning. In the *Second Treatise* (1689), natural rights to life, liberty, and property "have a foundation independent of the laws of any particular society." In the state of nature, "every one has the Executive Power of the Law of Nature" — each person has a natural right to judge and punish breaches. People enter civil society by resigning this natural power to the community, which becomes "umpire, by settled standing rules." But — and this is Locke's most consequential contribution — **when government dissolves or becomes tyrannical, rights revert to the people**: "all former ties are cancelled, all other rights cease, and every one has a right to defend himself." Locke's framework implicitly preserves the original meaning: when the institutional substitute for individual discernment fails, the discernment itself — the act of "pointing out" right from wrong — returns to individuals.

**Rousseau** introduces the paradox that most directly mirrors the etymological contradiction. The *Social Contract* (1762) argues that "the passage from the state of nature to the civil state produces a very remarkable change in man, by substituting justice for instinct." Yet his famous formulation — "whoever refuses to obey the general will shall be compelled to do so by the whole body; this means nothing less than that he will be **forced to be free**" — encodes the contradiction with unusual clarity. Individual discernment is overridden by collective discernment, and the individual who disagrees must accept this override as "freedom." Critics from Benjamin Constant to Isaiah Berlin have identified the totalitarian potential in this formulation; its relevance here is that it makes explicit what Hobbes conceals: the modern concept of justice requires the **active suppression** of individual discernment.

The natural law tradition of Grotius and Pufendorf resisted this suppression. Grotius in *De Jure Belli ac Pacis* (1625) identified self-defense, reparation, and punishment as just causes for individual action, maintaining that rights to enforce justice were "embedded in a natural law" existing prior to and independent of state authority. Pufendorf, while emphasizing humanity's social nature, articulated the prime natural law directive: "that no one should injure another and, if they have, that reparation should be made." For both thinkers, natural justice includes individual rights of discernment and self-help that are **logically prior** to the state. The state serves justice; it does not constitute it.

Under legal positivism — the dominant jurisprudential framework of modernity — this natural law resistance collapses. **Hans Kelsen's** *Pure Theory of Law* deliberately excludes the question of whether law is "just" as "not capable of scientific cognition." **H.L.A. Hart's** separability thesis holds that "it is in no sense a necessary truth that laws reproduce or satisfy certain demands of morality." The result is that "justice" within legal positivism **can only mean conformity with the legal system's own rules** — a circular definition that Gustav Radbruch's famous formula (1946) acknowledged breaks down at extremes: "an unjust law must be recognised as law, unless the conflict between statute and justice reaches such an intolerable degree" that the statute must yield. Radbruch formulated this in direct response to Nazi law — an extreme case that revealed the tautology's catastrophic potential.

## The surviving trace: self-help as vestigial etymological justice

Modern legal systems retain a narrow, carefully circumscribed space where the etymological meaning of justice survives: the doctrine of **self-help** (*Selbsthilfe*, 自助救済). The German Civil Code (BGB) §229 permits a person to seize property, arrest a fleeing debtor, or overcome resistance to an obligation **"if official assistance cannot be obtained in time and without immediate intervention there is a danger that the realization of the claim would be thwarted."** The provision is immediately hedged by §230 (obligation to seek judicial authorization afterward) and §231 (liability for mistaken self-help even without negligence). The structure is revealing: the state acknowledges that its monopoly sometimes fails, permits individuals to exercise discernment in those moments — and then immediately reasserts control.

This architecture encodes the contradiction precisely. Self-help exists as an exception proving the rule: the state's justice monopoly is the default; individual discernment is permitted only when the monopoly demonstrably fails; and even then, the state reserves the right to judge the individual's judgment after the fact. The etymological relationship is inverted: what was originally the primary meaning (individual discernment and action) survives only as a regulated exception to its own negation (state monopoly).

Carl Schmitt's concept of the *Ausnahmezustand* (state of exception) reveals the ultimate paradox. The sovereign, who monopolizes the "pointing out" of right, can decide to **suspend the pointing-out entirely**. The institution that claims exclusive authority over dikē can declare a condition in which dikē ceases to operate. Giorgio Agamben traces this to the Roman *justitium* — literally "standstill of law" — arguing that the exception has become the permanent horizon of modern sovereignty. The institution monopolizing "justice" thus possesses the power to negate justice itself, while still claiming the word.

The evidence from failed states provides empirical confirmation of the Lockean prediction: when the Weberian monopoly collapses, customary law, tribal justice, and individual self-help mechanisms spontaneously reassert themselves. This is not merely sociological observation but philosophical demonstration — the state's claim to constitute justice is **contingent, not necessary**. When the institutional framework dissolves, the deeper layer that the etymology preserves — individual and communal discernment about the proper order of things — resurfaces as if it had never disappeared.

## The principle hiding in the etymology

The PIE root *\*deik-* encodes a specific cognitive operation: the act of distinguishing, differentiating, pointing out one thing as opposed to another. The Routledge Encyclopedia of Philosophy captures the implication: "Rewards and punishments are justly distributed if they go to those who deserve them. **But in the absence of different desert claims, justice demands equal treatment.**" This formulation directly translates the etymological meaning into a principle: **when you can distinguish (discern relevant differences), justice requires differential treatment; when you cannot distinguish, justice requires equality.** Both operations presuppose the capacity for discernment — the very capacity the modern state expropriates.

Aristotle's distinction between distributive justice (fair allocation based on relevant differences) and corrective justice (rectifying wrongs by restoring balance) both require the act of dikē in its etymological sense: pointing out what distinguishes one case from another. The modern system claims to perform this discernment on behalf of individuals, but the claim is unstable. When the system produces outcomes that manifestly contradict discernment — when Radbruch's threshold is crossed — the contradiction becomes visible to everyone, and the etymological meaning reasserts itself as revolutionary right (Locke) or pure decisionism (Schmitt).

The argument is not that modern legal systems should be abolished in favor of private justice. It is that the word "justice" — carrying within itself three thousand years of meaning rooted in individual discernment, sacred formula, and cosmic order — has been pressed into service to name a system that systematically prohibits the very operations the word describes. This is not mere semantic drift. It is a structural contradiction that surfaces whenever the system fails: in Radbruch's formula, in Locke's right of revolution, in the BGB's self-help exception, in the spontaneous return of customary justice in failed states. The etymology does not merely record a historical curiosity. It preserves a philosophical truth that the modern system simultaneously depends upon and suppresses: that justice, at its root, is the human capacity to point out what is right — and that no institution can coherently claim to monopolize the act of pointing while prohibiting all others from using their hands.

## Conclusion: the contradiction as permanent instability

The etymological-philosophical analysis reveals that modern justice does not merely differ from its ancient meaning — it **negates** it. The PIE roots *\*deik-* ("to point out/distinguish"), *\*yewes-* ("sacred formula"), and *\*dheh₁-* ("to establish") all encode acts performed by individuals or small communities exercising discernment. The Mycenaean *ou themis*, Homer's competitive dikē, Hesiod's cosmic Dikē, Anaximander's natural dikē, and archaic Rome's *ius* as "approved self-help" all point in the same direction: justice was originally the name for the human capacity to discern and act upon what is right.

The modern system, consolidated through Roman codification, medieval *Landfrieden*, Westphalian sovereignty, and Weberian monopoly, uses this same word to name the **exclusive institutional prerogative** to perform that discernment — backed by the prohibition of anyone else performing it. The Hobbesian formulation makes the contradiction explicit: there is no justice without the sovereign, therefore justice is whatever the sovereign says. The word that meant "to point out" now names a system in which only one actor is permitted to point.

This contradiction is not merely theoretical. It generates three recurring phenomena in political life. First, the **Radbruch moment**: when state "justice" becomes sufficiently unjust, the etymological meaning forces itself back into consciousness, demanding that individuals exercise the discernment the system forbids. Second, the **Lockean reversion**: when the state fails entirely, the natural right to self-help — the original meaning of *ius* — reasserts itself as both philosophical principle and practical necessity. Third, the **Schmittian paradox**: the sovereign who monopolizes justice can suspend it, revealing that the monopoly was always a decision, not a natural fact.

The resolution lies not in choosing between individual and institutional justice but in recognizing that the tension between them is **constitutive** of the concept itself. Justice names both the capacity to discern and the institution that channels (and constrains) that capacity. The etymology preserves what the institution tries to forget: that its authority is borrowed from the very individual discernment it prohibits, and that this loan can be called in.
